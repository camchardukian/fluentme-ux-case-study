UX Design Case Study

FluentMe

A mobile app concept helping Vietnamese professionals practice English for real workplace situations.

Project Overview

FluentMe is a mobile app concept designed to help Vietnamese professionals prepare for, navigate, and reflect on English-language workplace conversations.

I developed the project across Cornell University’s User Experience Design certificate program, taking it through contextual research, affinity analysis, persona development, ideation, paper prototyping, high-fidelity prototyping, and usability testing.

UX researcher and product designer

The Problem and Target Users

FluentMe was designed for Vietnamese working professionals with beginner to lower-intermediate English skills.

Early research showed that participants often learned English reactively through workplace encounters, coworkers, videos, apps, or AI tools. Their learning was rarely organized around the vocabulary and communication situations they encountered at work.

This made it difficult to recall useful language during real conversations and contributed to nervousness, reduced confidence, and communication problems when speaking with foreign coworkers, clients, or patients.

Design challenge

How might we help Vietnamese professionals practice and apply relevant English before, during, and after real workplace conversations?

Research Findings

I conducted contextual interviews with Vietnamese professionals to understand how they learned English and handled communication challenges at work.

I reviewed the interview notes and observations, created activity notes, and organized them into an affinity diagram to identify repeated behaviors, frustrations, and needs.

View the full affinity diagram in FigJam

01

Learning was reactive

Participants frequently learned through unexpected workplace encounters, coworkers, videos, apps, or AI tools rather than following a structured plan connected to their professional goals.

02

Real usage mattered most

Practical conversations and workplace interactions appeared more useful for developing communication ability than studying generalized vocabulary and grammar in isolation.

03

Vocabulary gaps increased anxiety

Difficulty recalling relevant words or phrases made participants nervous and more likely to freeze or avoid speaking during workplace conversations.

From Sketches to Prototype

I explored multiple approaches before narrowing FluentMe into three complementary features that support users before, during, and after workplace conversations.

Early Ideation

These sketches explored meeting preparation, real-time conversational support, and extracting vocabulary from workplace documents.

Task Storyboards

The chosen concept supported users across three stages of workplace communication: preparation, real-time assistance, and reflection.

Initial Screen Sketches

I translated the selected concepts into initial screen sketches for the product’s three core features: preparing for a meeting, finding help during a conversation, and reviewing language afterward.

Revised Screen Sketches

I reviewed the initial screens and refined their content hierarchy, wording, and controls before building the interactive testing prototype.

Interactive Paper Prototype

I then developed the revised screens into a more detailed paper prototype for a formative testing session. A tablet-shaped frame and movable sticky notes simulated navigation, dropdowns, text entry, settings, feedback, and changing interface states.

Usability Testing and Iteration

I evaluated FluentMe through two rounds of testing, beginning with a formative paper-prototype session and later testing the interactive high-fidelity prototype with a user near the lower end of the target English-proficiency range.

The sessions helped identify problems that were not obvious from the screens alone, including unclear feature names, unnecessary cognitive load, missing response context, and prototype options that appeared usable but led to unfinished paths.

Round 1 · Paper prototype

Testing the concept and task flows

A remote formative feedback session tested the three primary features: preparing for a meeting, finding a professional reply during a conversation, and improving something the user previously said.

The participant saw the most value in Meeting Prep but found some labels and actions unclear. The session also showed that users needed to see their original practice response before reviewing suggested improvements.

Round 2 · High-fidelity prototype

Testing independent task completion

I conducted a 21-minute remote usability session in Vietnamese with a Vietnamese nurse whose English was approximately A1 level. She shared her screen while completing realistic workplace-language tasks in the Figma prototype.

This session exposed usability problems that were less visible with a stronger English speaker, particularly around English-only navigation and unsupported prototype options.

Changes Implemented

Implemented

Clearer feature names

“Safe Replies” became “Help Me Reply,” and “Conversation Review” became “Improve My Sentence.” The revised names describe the user’s goal more directly and reduce the chance of misinterpreting what each feature does.

Implemented

Smaller decision steps

Instead of immediately displaying a large list of phrases, Help Me Reply first asks whether the user needs someone to repeat, speak more slowly, or explain further. Suggested phrases appear only after that choice.

Implemented

Original response shown with feedback

The Meeting Prep feedback screen now includes the user’s original response before presenting corrections and a more natural version. This makes the feedback easier to understand and compare.

Implemented

Unfinished paths no longer look active

During the high-fidelity test, the participant selected an unsupported meeting option and could not continue. In the revised prototype, only the completed Client Meeting path is active, while future options are disabled and clearly marked “Coming soon.”

Future opportunity

The high-fidelity test also showed that an English-only interface can create too much navigation effort for users near the A1 level. A future version should offer an onboarding step or language setting that allows users to receive interface guidance in Vietnamese while still practicing English content.

Final Prototype

The final high-fidelity prototype brings the research findings into three focused tools that support users before, during, and after workplace conversations.

I prioritized a clear mobile interface, simple language, and short task flows that help users reach relevant workplace support without navigating through a traditional language course.

01

Meeting Prep

Users select a workplace meeting, review relevant words and phrases, practice a response, and receive feedback comparing what they said with clearer alternatives. This feature addresses participants’ need to prepare for workplace conversations before they happen.

02

Help Me Reply

Users choose whether they need someone to repeat, speak more slowly, or explain further, then receive polite phrases they can use immediately. It reduces the pressure of recalling language during stressful conversations.

03

Improve My Sentence

Users enter something they said or want to say and receive clearer, more natural workplace alternatives they can review and practice. It encourages learning from real conversations instead of studying generic examples.

Key Takeaways

FluentMe reinforced that useful UX work depends less on producing polished screens quickly and more on testing assumptions with the right users.

01

Test with the full target range

A design that feels straightforward to a stronger English speaker can still create major barriers for someone near the lower end of the intended proficiency range. Future studies should include participants representing each important user profile rather than relying on a single representative participant.

02

Prototype limitations must be explicit

Options that look interactive will be treated as interactive. Clearly disabling unfinished paths prevented users from entering dead ends and made the prototype more honest and easier to test.

03

Simple language is part of the interface

Labels, instructions, and navigation can become usability barriers when the product serves language learners. Clearer feature names and smaller decision steps improved comprehension without reducing functionality.

What I would do next

I would test the revised prototype with participants representing multiple English-proficiency levels, add Vietnamese interface guidance, and expand the currently supported workplace situations only after the core flows are consistently understood.
